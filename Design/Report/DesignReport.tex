\chapter{Abstract}
The Outreach and Research Build for Interactively Teaching Satellites, referred to as ORBITS herein, is a emulation system for a 1U CubeSat ecosystem including both a ground communication station and a physical ORBITS unit that provides a learning environment for both software and hardware concepts.

The ORBITS project is looking to develop a class based learning material that pupils, students, teachers and lecturers are able to gain hands on experience with to experiment, learn and research into CubeSat development. The ORBITS system looks to have modularity at the forefront of its design, allowing users to create their own payloads to interact with a stock unit to learn about different aspects of a CubeSat system and development. The system looks to be comprised of Commercial Off The Shelf (COTS) components where possible to keep costs low and allow for easy replacement of faulty parts.

Where applicable SysML diagrams have been used to represent the design of the ORBITS system to describe and provide an alternative method of understanding for users to reference.

\chapter{Introduction}

The Space industry around Scotland looks to increase the number of employees to \(20,000\) and targeting \pounds 4 billion in global market share by 2030~\cite{govScot}. To meet this growth within the industry high quality educational materials are needed to introduce young individuals to what they may be working on within the industry.

ORBITS, the Outreach and Research Build for Interactively Teaching Satellites, is a developmental tool for students to learn about satellite systems and their operation through physical experimentation and learning. The particular type of satellite being emulated by this device is a 1U CubeSat, a standardised type of nanosatellite that is becoming increasingly popular for its low cost and accessibility for companies and educational institutions to develop and launch their own satellites.

ORBITS provides both the software and hardware learning solutions to best prepare those looking for a career in the space industry and inspire those looking to learn about space and satellite systems. The system is designed to be modular allowing for users to create their own payloads to interact with a stock unit to learn about different aspects of a CubeSat system and development. COTS components are used throughout the design of this system to keep costs low and simplify maintenance. 

The system provides a seamless user experience from any standard laptop or desktop environment through a ground station that links to the ORBITS unit mimicking a real-world remote satellite communication network. This provides both a software development environment and a source for the learning materials for the system.

This report looks to cover a review of related work, requirement definition, hardware design, software design and prototype evaluation.

\chapter{Review of Related Work}

Firstly, it is important to understand what a CubeSat actually is and why they are important for the space industry. A CubeSat is a type of nanosatellite that is typically constructed in standard units of 10 cm x 10 cm x 11.35 cm, known as 1U. The reason for this standardisation is to allow for easier development and launch of these satellites as they can fit within a standardised deployment mechanism. These devices are typically launched to low orbit (\(<2000\) km \cite{LEO}).

CubeSats were first developed at the California Polytechnic State University, San Luis Obispo and Stanford University's Space Systems Development Lab in 1999 \cite{cubesat_org}. Using this initial work as a basis, the CubeSat standard was developed and released in 2004 with over 2900 CubeSat missions launched as of January 1st 2026 \cite{nanoSats_eu}. Shown in Figure \ref{fig:CubeSat_Missions} is a visual representation of the growth in CubeSat missions over time.

\begin{figure}[h]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=1\textwidth]{./Images/CubeSat_Missions.png}
    \caption{Growth in CubeSat Missions over time~\cite{nanoSats_eu}}
    \label{fig:CubeSat_Missions}
\end{figure}

This standard has allowed for the development of CubeSats to be more accessible for companies and educational institutions to develop and launch their own satellites. The CubeSat standard has also allowed for the development of a wide range of different types of CubeSats including those for Earth observation, communication, technology demonstration and scientific research.

CubeSats have become increasingly popular for their educational institutions due to the low cost nature of the development of these satellites with projects like the EduCube \cite{EduCube_1U} and UBO \cite{UBO} projects. These projects looked to provide Masters students hands on experience developing and learning with physical hardware based on usable standards within the industry. These projects have been successfully implemented into academia, and look to emulate every minute detail of a space ready model. This complexity relegates these types of projects for final year students with many hours of learning and research within the field.

To further increase the scope of teaching CubeSats a few criteria should be considered. The cost of the hardware, the amount of learning materials and the extensibility of functionality are all important when it comes to the development of such devices.

Products that look at the commercial and educational markets do exist on the market already, however they are often either very expensive, don't provide a ground station to satellite link or both. Take the Ukrainian made MySat for example~\cite{MySat}, a 1U CubeSat emulator that provides a physical unit for users to interact with and construct themselves. However it is quite expensive at around \pounds 300 for the kit targeted to universities and the main method of interaction is directly to the unit rather than from a ground station connection. This means that users are limited in the software development they can do with the unit and are not able to experience the full satellite development process.

Another example is the Australian made RASCube~\cite{RASCube}, a 1U CubeSat that provides field tested hardware within the design. The RASCube provides extensive learning materials and functionality but due to the nature of being designed from the ground up with completely custom PCBs the cost of a unit including all of the learning materials is around \pounds 1000 for a singular kit which may not be accessible for many educational institutions and individuals looking to learn about satellite systems. Despite this price point, the feature set and demand for high quality learning has driven more than 1400 kits sold \cite{RASCube}.

These products highlight a clear gap within the market: an affordable, ground-station linked CubeSat kit accessible to those with little to no prior experience, including first year undergraduates. ORBITS looks to provide an interactive teaching experience particularly focused on those with less experience to inspire and educate around the space industry.

Additional projects within space education do exist, such as the open source ISS Mimic~\cite{ISS-Mimic}, a project that looks to mimic the telemetry data from the International Space Station (ISS), using a 3D printed scale model, on board microcontroller and motors to provide a physical representation of the ISS. The data used to create this model is from a subset of the real ISS telemetry data and is publicly available through \href{https://lightstreamer.com/}{Lightstreamer}, an event broken used to deliver real time data to customers. 

\chapter{Design}
The ORBITS system has been developed using a top down approach starting with the high level requirements and breaking these down into smaller, more specific system requirements. The process first began by consulting the teaching staff most likely to be interacting with these systems within a classroom environment and seeing common sticking points with integrating new projects within the class, like time for set-up, ease of use during learning and both debugging of hardware and software related issues.

To begin, without a CubeSat emulation device, referred to as an ORBITS unit, there would be limited work that a student could perform. There needs to be some physical device that the user can interact with to be able to perform the relevant learning. This can then be decomposed into what subsystems/requirements need to be available within the ORBITS unit to be effective as both a learning tool while emulating a real-world CubeSat. These include,
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Power Management:} Within a CubeSat power needs to be constantly managed due to the harsh conditions and limited resources while in space. This includes the storage of on-board power and the distribution to each subsystem within the CubeSat.
    \item \textbf{Communication:} Some method to communicate between the ground station and the ORBITS unit that mimics the operation of an operational CubeSat including the protocol and data formatting.
    \item \textbf{Fitness to Form:} Allows the user to work with an industry-standard of the 1U form factor (10cm x 10cm x 11.35cm) while reducing the need for large models within the classroom.
    \item \textbf{On-Board Computer:} The on-board computer allows the system to operate and monitor the other components and subsystems. This can then allow for monitoring and telemetry data to be provided back to the user.
    \item \textbf{Attitude Determination and Control:} Within space a CubeSat is required to reorient itself to constantly optimise the power gain from the solar panels or provide optimal orientation for data collection from the payload. This then provides students with an introduction to control systems.
\end{itemize}

Another of the high level requirements is the use of COTS components. Commercial off the shelf, otherwise known as COTS, components are ready-made hardware and software solutions that are ready to be integrated into projects. In this case, COTS components can be leveraged to reduce the overall cost of an ORBITS unit by providing this ready-made solution. Additionally, by using COTS components design becomes more repairable as direct replacements can be sourced. This is beneficial where only small replacements within a unit are needed to be made rather than a full unit.

Experimentation facilitation was also required when considering the design of the ORBITS system. Without this the teaching capabilities of the system would be severely hindered defeating the purposes of creating ORBITS in the first place. Two main methods can be used to achieve this, both taking a different route. The hardware approach is allowing for a user defined payload to be integrated into the system. This allows for hands on experience that a student can get the satisfaction of building themselves. The software approach is allowing the ORBITS unit to be reprogrammable to allow users to interact with the system in different, unique ways.

Finally, data processing and visualisation is required for this system. Without it the user wouldn't be able to get feedback from the system they have constructed, reducing the effective learning achievable from this system. To enable the data processing and visualisation, the ground station will be a device capable of hosting and sharing its own web-based application to allow the user to interact with the system.

The ESP32-S3 was selected for the OBC due to its low cost, high performance and wide range of integrated connectivity with simple development through the Arduino Integrated Development Environment (IDE) opening learning material to many open source libraries for components and features. 

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Electrical interface:} 3.3~V input, 500~mA max current, overcurrent protection. Low power with on-board buck converter for voltage conversion from 5V input to required 3.3V input.
    \item \textbf{Data interface:} UART, I2C, SPI, WiFi 802.11 b/g/n, Bluetooth LE 5.0. These allow for different connections to be made to the microcontroller meaning many devices can be integrated into the base system along with payloads. Also allows for wireless reprogramming through a local area network connection.
    \item \textbf{Mechanical interface:} 25.4 mm x 62.74 mm board, 38 GPIO pins. Plenty of connectivity for control of the base unit and user defined payloads all within a form factor that fits within the designed frame.
    \item \textbf{Software interface:} ESP-IDF (C/C++), Arduino IDE support, PlatformIO support, FreeRTOS-based OS, extensive peripheral libraries.
\end{itemize}

The specific variant chosen was the ESP32-S3-N8R8-1U which has 8MB of both flash memory and PSRAM. This variant also allows for the connecting of an external antenna through an IPEX connection to increase the range for the on-board WiFi and Bluetooth while reducing the size of the pcb. Of particular note with this microcontroller is the addition of 2 I2C busses. I2C is a popular synchronous 2 wire communication protocol that can allow for several devices to be controlled through minimal pins connected to the controller. Each device connected to the bus has an address defined by the manufacturer that can be used to interact with the components registers, usually to access the data contained within a sensor or to set component characteristics. By having 2 of these I2C busses included within the device it can allow for internal communications to be handled by one while the other is purely used for communications to the user defined payload.

In addition to the on board computer a PCF8523 breakout board is included within the system. This is to provide a highly accurate external clock signal external to that of the microcontroller using a \(32.768 kHz\) quartz crystal. The reason for this addition into the system is because of the unreliability with the application. Using the internal clock can lead to a few issues, namely inaccuracies in timing, device reset causes the epoch time to reset to zero and, finally, the timings can drift depending on temperature of the environment.

To mitigate all of these issues, an external real-time clock signal can be provided, in this situation a PCF8523. This external clock is very cost effective in an ORBITS unit as it is much cheaper than the alternatives. However, at this price point the PCF8523 exhibits a drift of approximately 2 seconds per day. This drawback does not affect the operation of an ORBITS unit as operation may only last from a few seconds to a few minutes with rapid developments making this external clock very cost effective.

\subsection{Communications Subsystem}\label{communications_subsystem}

Communications are integral for the operations of this CubeSat system. Implemented within the system are 2 forms of inter-device communication. WiFi, being one of them, is included with both the ground station and OBC with the ground station being able to generate its own WiFi network that the OBC can connect to.

With WiFi being included in both the ground station and OBC, this allows for a simple method of communication between the two devices. The WiFi connection can be used to send commands from the ground station to the OBC and receive telemetry data back from the OBC. This allows for a seamless user experience as users can interact with the ORBITS unit from any standard laptop or desktop environment without the need for additional hardware or software.

However, within CubeSat development WiFi is not the best analogue for space communication. To give an alternative communication method more in line with a CubeSat a wireless RF transceiver module has been included with both the ground station and ORBITS unit.

\subsection{Attitude Determination and Control Subsystem (ADCS)}

The attitude determination and control subsystem (ADCS) is responsible for determining the orientation of the ORBITS unit and providing control to adjust the orientation as needed. This is particularly important for CubeSats as they need to orient themselves to optimise the power gain from the solar panels or provide the required orientation for data collection from the payload. The ADCS subsystem needs to be able to provide accurate attitude determination and control while also being low power and cost effective. 

Rather than implementing a full ADCS system with reaction wheels and magnetorquers, the ORBITS unit looks to focus on the determination aspect of the ADCS subsystem as to reduce the number of components needed within the system and to allow for a more cost effective solution while giving electronics representative to those within a typical CubeSat. The attitude determination can be achieved through the use of an inertial measurement unit (IMU), a type of composite sensor comprised of accelerometers, gyroscopes and magnetometers.

The IMU chosen for the system was the ICM-20948, a low cost 9-axis IMU. At the price point for the ORBITS system it is typical to find components that aren't as feature dense as more expensive counterparts. In this case, IMU's typically reduce down to 3 or 6 axis variants, with only accelerometers or gyroscopes, for instance the LSM6DS3TR only has 6-axis, 3 for acceleration and 3 for the gyroscope. The ICM-20948, for the same price manages to contain a magnetometer in addition to the accelerometer and gyroscope.

This addition is particularly useful within space applications. Without a magnetometer, the only method that can determine the orientation of the satellite is through the gyroscope. However gyroscopes run into an issue when running for longer periods of time, this being drift. Small uncertainties within the readings build up over time causing the output to be untrustworthy. Unlike the readings from the gyroscope, the magnetometer has an external reference. In the case of typical CubeSats, this is the Earth. By reading the magnetic fields produced by the Earth the sensor is able to calibrate itself relative to North which can be used to correct the drift from the gyroscope and provide a more accurate reading of the orientation of the satellite.

The ground station needs to facilitate multiple of the requirements needed for the system to be successful with particular focus on the educational element. The ground station is required to provide the link from a standard laptop or desktop environment to be able to learn about programming within the context of a CubeSat.

The Raspberry Pi Zero 2W is a versatile single board computer that can have a wide range of different applications, in this case acting as the link between the users' development device and the ORBITS unit. With the versatility of such a device a wide range of different approaches can be taken to solve the ground station as many different design methodologies, frameworks and technologies that exist that can come together with differing results.

Commonly, Application Programming Interfaces (APIs) are used as a method for allowing different software components to interact with each other and is used within the ground station to allow for the frontend and backend to communicate with each other but there are several approaches that can be taken to implement an API in a greater tech stack.

For instance, the MEAN framework (MongoDB, Express.js, Angular and Node.js) is an open source JavaScript framework that is mainly used for cloud based web applications~\cite{MEAN}. Flask is a lightweight web framework that can be used to create a RESTful API~\cite{REST_API} for the ground station while React or AntDesign can be used to create a user interface that interacts with the API. \subsubsection{Backend}

The backend architecture for the ground station is built using several components with Flask being the main web framework used to create a RESTful API for the ground station. The reason Flask is used rather than other options like Django or FastAPI because of it simplistic flexibility. It is a lightweight web application framework that enables rapid development and allows for features to be added when needed. The ORBITS dashboard is a simplistic web application with minimal traffic meaning the minimal feature set that a standard flask installation is able to provide all of the functionality required. But this doesn't mean it can't be improved through the added functionality, namely SocketIO.

SocketIO is a library that enables real-time, bidirectional communication between the client and the server. It uses WebSockets as the underlying transport protocol and provides a simple API for handling real-time events. This is particularly useful for the ORBITS system as it allows for real-time updates to the user interface based on events that occur in the backend, such as receiving telemetry data from the ORBITS unit or sending commands to the unit. By using SocketIO, this can allow for more simple development of the frontend as it can listen for events from the backend and update the user interface accordingly without the need for complex polling mechanisms or manual updates.

Similarly to how SocketIO allows for real-time communication between the frontend and backend, Message Queuing Telemetry Transport (MQTT) is used for communications between the ground station and the ORBITS unit. MQTT is a lightweight messaging protocol that is designed for the use in low bandwidth and high latency environments. In this case it is used to connect the two devices through a wireless hotspot created by the ground station. This allows for the ground station to send commands to the ORBITS unit and receive telemetry data back from the unit in real-time, providing a seamless user experience when interacting with the ORBITS system. The use of MQTT requires another service to be running on the Raspberry Pi to handle the MQTT messages, in this case the Mosquitto MQTT broker~\cite{Mosquitto} is used to handle the MQTT messages between the ground station and the ORBITS unit.

In addition to the MQTT broker, to facilitate a communication method more in line with that of a CubeSat, a driver for the NRF24L01+ RF transceiver is included within the backend. This allows for the ground station to communicate with the ORBITS unit through the NRF24L01+ module in addition to the WiFi connection. This provides a more authentic experience for users as they can interact with the ORBITS unit through a method that is more similar to that of a true CubeSat.

By having both the MQTT broker and the NRF24L01+ driver within the backend architecture, this allows for the ground station to have multiple methods of communication with the ORBITS unit. This can provide a more robust and reliable communication system as if one method fails, the other can still be used to communicate with the ORBITS unit. Additionally, this allows for users to choose which method they want to use to communicate with the ORBITS unit, providing more flexibility in how they interact with the system.

In both cases of the MQTT broker and the NRF24L01+ driver, a message protocol needs to be defined to allow for the communication between the ground station and the ORBITS unit to be structured and organised. ArduinoJson is a library that allows for the serialization and deserialization of JSON data in Arduino compatible microcontrollers. This is used to create a message protocol based on the database schema for the telemetry data and system events. This allows for the messages sent between the ground station and the ORBITS unit to be structured in a way that can be easily parsed and stored in the database, providing a seamless integration between the communication methods and the database.

Another major component of the backend architecture is the use of PlatformIO, an open source embedded software development platform that can be run from the command line. This is used to compile and upload the user defined code for the payload to the OBC microcontroller on the ORBITS unit through a feature called Over The Air (OTA) updates, which allows for the code to be uploaded wirelessly through the WiFi connection between the ground station and the ORBITS unit. This allows for users to easily upload their code to the ORBITS unit without needing to physically connect to the device, providing a more seamless user experience when developing their own payloads for the ORBITS system. An alternative to this would be to use the Arduino CLI which provides similar functionality but is not as fully featured as PlatformIO and may require more manual configuration to get working with the ORBITS system including the need to set up an OTA update mechanism manually rather than having it built into the platform.

\subsubsection{Frontend}

The frontend architecture for the ground station is built using React as the main framework for building the user interface. React provides a component-based architecture that allows for the creation of reusable UI components, making it easier to manage the complexity of the user interface. In addition to React, several libraries and frameworks are used to enhance the functionality of the frontend.

AntDesign is one such React UI library that provides a wide range of pre-built components and styles. It allows for the creation of a modern responsive user interface with simple extensibility. It allows for the rapid prototyping of the user interface to allow for more iterative development to occur. But this is mainly to provide a better user experience for users interacting with the ORBITS system. Without an engaging and intuitive user interface, users may not be as inclined to interact with the system and potentially not reach the intended learning outcomes. 

For the education programming of the ORBITS system this is also an important factor. Without an engaging and intuitive development environment users may find it difficult to actively learn about the concepts. To make the system as intuitive as possible, Blockly \cite{Blockly} is used to provide a block based development environment for users to create their own code to interact with the ORBITS unit. Blockly allows for users to create code by dragging and dropping blocks that represent different pre-defined programming elements. This can be particularly useful for users who are new to programming or prefer a visual approach to coding. However Blockly is only the visual programming environment and doesn't provide a way for users to see the code being generated by the blocks. To bridge this gap, the Monaco Editor is used to provide a code editor that displays the code being generated by the Blockly editor. This allows users to see the code being generated by the blocks in a text format that can be used for debugging and to bridge the gap between the visual programming and traditional text based programming. Shown in Figure~\ref{fig:blockly_monaco} is the Blockly visual programming environment with the Monaco code editor displaying the generated code.

As this is an educational system, it is important to provide users with the ability to perform some form of analysis on the telemetry data received from the ORBITS unit. This allows users to gain insights into the performance of their payloads and understand how different components of the system interact with each other. Two methods are used to provide this functionality, the first being through the use of ChartJs, a JavaScript library that allows for the creation of interactive charts and graphs. This is used during runtime of an ORBITS unit mission to provide real-time visualisation of the telemetry data being received from the unit. The other method is through allowing the export of the telemetry data in a CSV format that can be used for offline analysis. This allows users to perform more in depth analysis on the telemetry data using tools like Excel or Python, providing a more comprehensive learning experience when interacting with the ORBITS system.

\subsubsection{Database}
%Mimicking actual telemetry data from the ISS mainly from formatting and include schema for the database and phpmyadmin. 
The database within the ground station is used to store general system operations and telemetry from the ORBITS unit, rather than storing it on board. The database contains 3 separate tables:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{System Events:} General operations of the device, for instance changing operating mode, charging state or error.
    \item \textbf{Telemetry Updates:} Recurring data coming from the onboard sensors, Not as frequent as that of the ADCS. For instance, voltage and current draw or Payload readings.
    \item \textbf{ADCS Data:} Used to store frequent readings from the Attitude Determination and Control system 
\end{itemize}

These tables follow a similar structure to that of the telemetry data from the ISS. A subset of this data is available through both the ISS Mimic~\cite{ISS-Mimic} project and \href{https://lightstreamer.com/}{Lightstreamer}, and this informed the design of the database schema for the ORBITS system to provide a better analogue to the data that would be received from a real CubeSat. 

The system events tables is structured as such:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{ID:} Standard automatic incrementing integer primary key.
    \item \textbf{Mission ID:} Unique Identifier to distinguish user variants with different code implementations within the structure.
    \item \textbf{Event Type:} General category in which an operation has occurred like payload operation.
    \item \textbf{Description:} Short description of the operation occurred.
    \item \textbf{Timestamp:} Time at which the operation occurred from on board real-time clock.
\end{itemize}

The telemetry updates table is structured as such:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{ID:} Standard auto increment primary key.
    \item \textbf{Mission ID:} Unique Identifier to distinguish different code implementations.
    \item \textbf{Voltage:} The voltage of the system at a given time.
    \item \textbf{Current:} The current draw of the system at a given time.
    \item \textbf{Power:} The power consumption of the system at a given time.
    \item \textbf{Timestamp:} The corresponding time measured by the on board real-time clock.
\end{itemize}

Finally, the ADCS updates has its own table structured differently. This is to service high request volumes in comparison to the other tables.

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{ID:} Standard auto increment primary key.
    \item \textbf{Mission ID:} Unique Identifier to distinguish different code implementations.
    \item \textbf{Acceleration:} 3 components each corresponding to the acceleration in each axis.
    \item \textbf{Gyroscope:} 3 components representing the Roll, Pitch, and Yaw of the ORBITS unit at a given time.
    \item \textbf{Magnetometer:} 3 components representing the magnetic field strength in each of the 3 axis.
    \item \textbf{Timestamp:} The corresponding time measured by the on board real-time clock.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{ORBITS Unit Firmware}
%Using standard libraries for the components to make development as simple as possible.
The base software used for the ORBITS unit provides the necessary functionality to operate the components within the system and provide a platform for users to integrate their own code for their payloads. After the user has created their own code for their payload, the code is sent to the ground station for compiling using the PlatformIO and wirelessly transmitted to the ORBITS unit through the WiFi connection. The code is then flashed onto the OBC microcontroller and can subsequently be executed on launch of the ORBITS unit. 

The ESP-32 microcontroller uses the FreeROTS real-time operating system (RTOS) to manage the different tasks and processes running on the device, for instance the getting the telemetry data from the sensors on the I2C bus and providing them to the MQTT broker. This allows for the efficient use of resources and provides a more responsive experience when interacting with the ORBITS unit. The RTOS allows for the scheduling of tasks and provides mechanisms for inter-task communication, allowing for the different components within the system to work together seamlessly.

In conjunction with the FreeRTOS the software within the ORBITS unit makes use of the Arduino framework and libraries for the components used within the system. This allows for a more user friendly experience as users can make use of the existing documentation and learning materials for these components when creating their own payloads. The software is structured in a way to allow for easy integration of user defined code for payloads while still providing the necessary functionality to operate the system.

Each of the components within the system, if available, have an open source library available for the Arduino framework which allows for a more straightforward development process. For instance, the ICM-20948 IMU has a library provided by Adafruit that allows for easy access to the sensor data and configuration of the sensor. This allows for users to easily integrate the sensor data into their payloads without needing to worry about the low level details of how to communicate with the sensor. These functions are used as the entry point for the OBRITS unit firmware and initialise the internal components of the system and run the main loop of the program respectively. The user defined code for the payloads can then be added after these function calls to allow for the user code to run alongside the base software for the ORBITS unit. This allows for users to easily integrate their own code for their payloads while still being able to make use of the existing functionality provided by the base software, providing a seamless integration between the user defined code and the base software for the ORBITS unit.

Within the ORBITS\_setup function the internal and external communications are set up, for instance the I2C bus for the sensors and the WiFi connection for the MQTT broker. This allows for the necessary communication channels to be established. The ORBITS\_loop function is responsible for reading the sensor data from the ICM-20948 IMU and the INA260 power monitor and sending this data to the MQTT broker to be stored in the database and visualised on the dashboard. This allows for the telemetry data from the ORBITS unit to be sent to the ground station in real-time, providing a seamless user experience when interacting with the ORBITS system.

For the telemetry data to be sent to the ground station, the data is read from the sensors and then formatted into a JSON object using the ArduinoJson library. This allows for the data to be structured in a way that can be easily parsed and stored in the database on the ground station. The JSON object is then sent to the MQTT broker through the WiFi connection and subsequently handled by the backend.

\section{Future Work}

Due to the scope of the project, there are several areas that could be expanded upon in the future to further enhance the ORBITS system as in some areas the design and implementation of the system is focused on providing a minimum viable product to meet the requirements of the project. Some areas that could be expanded upon in the future include:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Attitude Control:} The current design of the ORBITS unit focuses on the attitude determination aspect of the ADCS subsystem. In the future, this could be expanded to include attitude control through the use of reaction wheels or magnetorquers to provide a more complete ADCS system.
    \item \textbf{Ground Station Software:} The current design of the ground station software provides a basic user interface for interacting with the ORBITS unit. In the future, this could be expanded to include additional features such as data visualization tools, more advanced telemetry analysis and support for multiple users interacting with the system simultaneously.
    \item \textbf{Solar Panels:} The current design of the ORBITS unit includes support for solar charging through the power management module, but does not include actual solar panels. In the future, solar panels could be added to the design to allow for more authentic charging of the ORBITS unit and to provide a more complete experience for users.
    \item \textbf{Improved Dashboard:} The current design of the dashboard provides a basic interface for users to interact with the ORBITS unit and view telemetry data. In the future, the dashboard could also be used as a control interface for users to send commands defined by the user to the ORBITS unit to control the system and interact with their payloads in real-time, providing a more engaging and interactive experience for users.
    \item \textbf{ORBITS Unit Firmware Optimisation:} The current design of the ORBITS unit firmware provides the necessary functionality to operate the system but heavily relies on the use of libraries for the components within the system with possible repeat functionality across the different libraries for instance, the ICM-20948 library and the INA260 library both have some method of reading data from each individual sensor on the I2C bus, while a more general approach can allow each sensor to be addressed and read in a more efficient way, reducing the overhead of the firmware and allowing for more resources to be available for user defined code for payloads.
    \item \textbf{Complete NRF24L01+ OTA:} The current design of the ORBITS unit allows for OTA updates through the WiFi connection but does not currently support OTA updates through the NRF24L01+ module. In the future, this could be implemented to allow for users to upload their code to the ORBITS unit through a more authentic communication method, providing a more complete experience for users.
    \item \textbf{Enhance Educational Programming:} Additional blocks can be added to the Blockly editor to allow for more complex code to be generated for user defined payloads, allowing for a wider range of educational material to be covered through the ORBITS system. For instance, blocks that interact with specific payloads could be added to allow for users to easilty integrate new payloads and make it more accessible for users to create their own payloads without needing to worry about the low level details of how to interact with the components within the ORBITS unit.
\end{itemize}

What can also be taken from this project is the potential for future iterations of the ORBITS system. The design and implementation of the current ORBITS system provide a solid foundation that can be built further upon to widen the scope of teaching available through the system. For instance, the current design of the ORBITS unit is focused on providing a platform for users to learn about programming within the context of a CubeSat, but future iterations of the system could expand to cover other aspects of CubeSat design and operation such as thermal management or satellite communication systems. This would allow for a more comprehensive learning experience for users and provide a more complete understanding of the different subsystems that make up a CubeSat.

The implementation of the ORBITS system also provides a platform for other emulatory devices to be developed in the future. For instance, keeping within the space theme, a similar system could be developed to emulate the operations of a Mars rover or a lunar lander, as many of the systems constructed for the ORBITS unit could be repurposed for these types of systems. This would allow for a wider range of educational material to be covered with the same underlying principles of providing a platform for users to learn about programming and system design within the context of space exploration.

\section{Reflection}

As it currently stands, the ORBITS system provides a solid foundation for users to learn about programming within the context of a CubeSat. The design and implementation of the system meet the requirements set out at the beginning of the project and provide a platform for users to create their own payloads and interact with the ORBITS system in a meaningful way.
