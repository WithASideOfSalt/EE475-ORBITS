\chapter{Abstract}
The Outreach and Research Build for Interactively Teaching Satellites, referred to as ORBITS herein, is a emulation system for a 1U CubeSat ecosystem including both a ground communication station and a physical ORBITS unit that provides a learning environment for both software and hardware concepts.

The ORBITS project is looking to develop a class based learning material that pupils, students, teachers and lecturers are able to gain hands on experience with to experiment, learn and research into CubeSat development. The ORBITS system looks to have modularity at the forefront of its design, allowing users to create their own payloads to interact with a stock unit to learn about different aspects of a CubeSat system and development. The system looks to be comprised of Commercial Off The Shelf (COTS) components where possible to keep costs low and allow for easy replacement of faulty parts.

Where applicable SysML diagrams have been used to represent the design of the ORBITS system to describe and provide an alternative method of understanding for users to reference.

\chapter{Introduction}

The Space industry around Scotland looks to increase the number of employees to \(20,000\) and targeting \pounds 4 billion in global market share by 2030~\cite{govScot}. To meet this growth within the industry high quality educational materials are needed to introduce young individuals to what they may be working on within the industry.

ORBITS, the Outreach and Research Build for Interactively Teaching Satellites, is a developmental tool for students to learn about satellite systems and their operation through physical experimentation and learning. The particular type of satellite being emulated by this device is a 1U CubeSat, a standardised type of nanosatellite that is becoming increasingly popular for its low cost and accessibility for companies and educational institutions to develop and launch their own satellites.

ORBITS provides both the software and hardware learning solutions to best prepare those looking for a career in the space industry and inspire those looking to learn about space and satellite systems. The system is designed to be modular allowing for users to create their own payloads to interact with a stock unit to learn about different aspects of a CubeSat system and development. COTS components are used throughout the design of this system to keep costs low and simplify maintenance. 

The system provides a seamless user experience from any standard laptop or desktop environment through a ground station that links to the ORBITS unit mimicking a real-world remote satellite communication network. This provides both a software development environment and a source for the learning materials for the system.

This report looks to cover a review of related work, requirement definition, hardware design, software design and prototype evaluation.

\chapter{Review of Related Work}

Firstly, it is important to understand what a CubeSat actually is and why they are important for the space industry. A CubeSat is a type of nanosatellite that is typically constructed in standard units of 10 cm x 10 cm x 11.35 cm, known as 1U. The reason for this standardisation is to allow for easier development and launch of these satellites as they can fit within a standardised deployment mechanism. These devices are typically launched to low orbit (\(<2000\) km \cite{LEO}).

CubeSats were first developed at the California Polytechnic State University, San Luis Obispo and Stanford University's Space Systems Development Lab in 1999 \cite{cubesat_org}. Using this initial work as a basis, the CubeSat standard was developed and released in 2004 with over 2900 CubeSat missions launched as of January 1st 2026 \cite{nanoSats_eu}. Shown in Figure \ref{fig:CubeSat_Missions} is a visual representation of the growth in CubeSat missions over time.

\begin{figure}[h]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=1\textwidth]{./Images/CubeSat_Missions.png}
    \caption{Growth in CubeSat Missions over time~\cite{nanoSats_eu}}
    \label{fig:CubeSat_Missions}
\end{figure}

This standard has allowed for the development of CubeSats to be more accessible for companies and educational institutions to develop and launch their own satellites. The CubeSat standard has also allowed for the development of a wide range of different types of CubeSats including those for Earth observation, communication, technology demonstration and scientific research.

CubeSats have become increasingly popular for their educational institutions due to the low cost nature of the development of these satellites with projects like the EduCube \cite{EduCube_1U} and UBO \cite{UBO} projects. These projects looked to provide Masters students hands on experience developing and learning with physical hardware based on usable standards within the industry. These projects have been successfully implemented into academia, and look to emulate every minute detail of a space ready model. This complexity relegates these types of projects for final year students with many hours of learning and research within the field.

To further increase the scope of teaching CubeSats a few criteria should be considered. The cost of the hardware, the amount of learning materials and the extensibility of functionality are all important when it comes to the development of such devices.

Products that look at the commercial and educational markets do exist on the market already, however they are often either very expensive, don't provide a ground station to satellite link or both. Take the Ukrainian made MySat for example~\cite{MySat}, a 1U CubeSat emulator that provides a physical unit for users to interact with and construct themselves. However it is quite expensive at around \pounds 300 for the kit targeted to universities and the main method of interaction is directly to the unit rather than from a ground station connection. This means that users are limited in the software development they can do with the unit and are not able to experience the full satellite development process.

Another example is the Australian made RASCube~\cite{RASCube}, a 1U CubeSat that provides field tested hardware within the design. The RASCube provides extensive learning materials and functionality but due to the nature of being designed from the ground up with completely custom PCBs the cost of a unit including all of the learning materials is around \pounds 1000 for a singular kit which may not be accessible for many educational institutions and individuals looking to learn about satellite systems. Despite this price point, the feature set and demand for high quality learning has driven more than 1400 kits sold \cite{RASCube}.

These products highlight a clear gap within the market: an affordable, ground-station linked CubeSat kit accessible to those with little to no prior experience, including first year undergraduates. ORBITS looks to provide an interactive teaching experience particularly focused on those with less experience to inspire and educate around the space industry.

Additional projects within space education do exist, such as the open source ISS Mimic~\cite{ISS-Mimic}, a project that looks to mimic the telemetry data from the International Space Station (ISS), using a 3D printed scale model, on board microcontroller and motors to provide a physical representation of the ISS. The data used to create this model is from a subset of the real ISS telemetry data and is publicly available through \href{https://lightstreamer.com/}{Lightstreamer}, an event broken used to deliver real time data to customers. 

\chapter{Design}
The ORBITS system has been developed using a top down approach starting with the high level requirements and breaking these down into smaller, more specific system requirements. The process first began by consulting the teaching staff most likely to be interacting with these systems within a classroom environment and seeing common sticking points with integrating new projects within the class, like time for set-up, ease of use during learning and both debugging of hardware and software related issues.

To begin, without a CubeSat emulation device, referred to as an ORBITS unit, there would be limited work that a student could perform. There needs to be some physical device that the user can interact with to be able to perform the relevant learning. This can then be decomposed into what subsystems/requirements need to be available within the ORBITS unit to be effective as both a learning tool while emulating a real-world CubeSat. These include,
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Power Management:} Within a CubeSat power needs to be constantly managed due to the harsh conditions and limited resources while in space. This includes the storage of on-board power and the distribution to each subsystem within the CubeSat.
    \item \textbf{Communication:} Some method to communicate between the ground station and the ORBITS unit that mimics the operation of an operational CubeSat including the protocol and data formatting.
    \item \textbf{Fitness to Form:} Allows the user to work with an industry-standard of the 1U form factor (10cm x 10cm x 11.35cm) while reducing the need for large models within the classroom.
    \item \textbf{On-Board Computer:} The on-board computer allows the system to operate and monitor the other components and subsystems. This can then allow for monitoring and telemetry data to be provided back to the user.
    \item \textbf{Attitude Determination and Control:} Within space a CubeSat is required to reorient itself to constantly optimise the power gain from the solar panels or provide optimal orientation for data collection from the payload. This then provides students with an introduction to control systems.
\end{itemize}

Another of the high level requirements is the use of COTS components. Commercial off the shelf, otherwise known as COTS, components are ready-made hardware and software solutions that are ready to be integrated into projects. In this case, COTS components can be leveraged to reduce the overall cost of an ORBITS unit by providing this ready-made solution. Additionally, by using COTS components design becomes more repairable as direct replacements can be sourced. This is beneficial where only small replacements within a unit are needed to be made rather than a full unit.

Experimentation facilitation was also required when considering the design of the ORBITS system. Without this the teaching capabilities of the system would be severely hindered defeating the purposes of creating ORBITS in the first place. Two main methods can be used to achieve this, both taking a different route. The hardware approach is allowing for a user defined payload to be integrated into the system. This allows for hands on experience that a student can get the satisfaction of building themselves. The software approach is allowing the ORBITS unit to be reprogrammable to allow users to interact with the system in different, unique ways.

Finally, data processing and visualisation is required for this system. Without it the user wouldn't be able to get feedback from the system they have constructed, reducing the effective learning achievable from this system. To enable the data processing and visualisation, the ground station will be a device capable of hosting and sharing its own web-based application to allow the user to interact with the system.

The ESP32-S3 was selected for the OBC due to its low cost, high performance and wide range of integrated connectivity with simple development through the Arduino Integrated Development Environment (IDE) opening learning material to many open source libraries for components and features. 

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Electrical interface:} 3.3~V input, 500~mA max current, overcurrent protection. Low power with on-board buck converter for voltage conversion from 5V input to required 3.3V input.
    \item \textbf{Data interface:} UART, I2C, SPI, WiFi 802.11 b/g/n, Bluetooth LE 5.0. These allow for different connections to be made to the microcontroller meaning many devices can be integrated into the base system along with payloads. Also allows for wireless reprogramming through a local area network connection.
    \item \textbf{Mechanical interface:} 25.4 mm x 62.74 mm board, 38 GPIO pins. Plenty of connectivity for control of the base unit and user defined payloads all within a form factor that fits within the designed frame.
    \item \textbf{Software interface:} ESP-IDF (C/C++), Arduino IDE support, FreeRTOS-based OS, extensive peripheral libraries.
\end{itemize}

There are several variants of the ESP32-S3, with variants having different amounts of flash memory and Pseudo-Static RAM (PSRAM) and on board antenna. The specific variant chosen was the ESP32-S3-N8R8-1U which has 8MB of both flash memory and PSRAM. This variant also allows for the connecting of an external antenna through an IPEX connection to increase the range for the on-board WiFi and Bluetooth while reducing the size of the pcb. Of particular note with this microcontroller is the addition of 2 I2C busses. I2C is a popular synchronous 2 wire communication protocol that can allow for several devices to be controlled through minimal pins connected to the controller. Each device connected to the bus has an address defined by the manufacturer that can be used to interact with the components registers, usually to access the data contained within a sensor or to set component characteristics. By having 2 of these I2C busses included within the device it can allow for internal communications to be handled by one while the other is purely used for communications to the user defined payload.

In addition to the on board computer a PCF8523 breakout board is included within the system. This is to provide a highly accurate external clock signal external to that of the microcontroller using a \(32.768 kHz\) quartz crystal. The reason for this addition into the system is because of the unreliability with the application. Using the internal clock can lead to a few issues, namely inaccuracies in timing, device reset causes the epoch time to reset to zero and, finally, the timings can drift depending on temperature of the environment.

To mitigate all of these issues, an external real-time clock signal can be provided, in this situation a PCF8523. This external clock is very cost effective in an ORBITS unit as it is much cheaper than the alternatives. However, at this price point the PCF8523 exhibits a drift of approximately 2 seconds per day. This drawback does not affect the operation of an ORBITS unit as operation may only last from a few seconds to a few minutes with rapid developments making this external clock very cost effective.

\subsection{Communications Subsystem}\label{communications_subsystem}

Communications are integral for the operations of this CubeSat system. Implemented within the system are 2 forms of inter-device communication. WiFi, being one of them, is included with both the ground station and OBC with the ground station being able to generate its own WiFi network that the OBC can connect to.

With WiFi being included in both the ground station and OBC, this allows for a simple method of communication between the two devices. The WiFi connection can be used to send commands from the ground station to the OBC and receive telemetry data back from the OBC. This allows for a seamless user experience as users can interact with the ORBITS unit from any standard laptop or desktop environment without the need for additional hardware or software.

However, within CubeSat development WiFi is not the best analogue for space communication. To give an alternative communication method more in line with a CubeSat a wireless RF transceiver module has been included with both the ground station and ORBITS unit.

\subsection{Attitude Determination and Control Subsystem (ADCS)}

The attitude determination and control subsystem (ADCS) is responsible for determining the orientation of the ORBITS unit and providing control to adjust the orientation as needed. This is particularly important for CubeSats as they need to orient themselves to optimise the power gain from the solar panels or provide the required orientation for data collection from the payload. The ADCS subsystem needs to be able to provide accurate attitude determination and control while also being low power and cost effective. 

Rather than implementing a full ADCS system with reaction wheels and magnetorquers, the ORBITS unit looks to focus on the determination aspect of the ADCS subsystem as to reduce the number of components needed within the system and to allow for a more cost effective solution while giving electronics representative to those within a typical CubeSat. The attitude determination can be achieved through the use of an inertial measurement unit (IMU), a type of composite sensor comprised of accelerometers, gyroscopes and magnetometers.

The IMU chosen for the system was the ICM-20948, a low cost 9-axis IMU. At the price point for the ORBITS system it is typical to find components that aren't as feature dense as more expensive counterparts. In this case, IMU's typically reduce down to 3 or 6 axis variants, with only accelerometers or gyroscopes, for instance the LSM6DS3TR only has 6-axis, 3 for acceleration and 3 for the gyroscope. The ICM-20948, for the same price manages to contain a magnetometer in addition to the accelerometer and gyroscope.

This addition is particularly useful within space applications. Without a magnetometer, the only method that can determine the orientation of the satellite is through the gyroscope. However gyroscopes run into an issue when running for longer periods of time, this being drift. Small uncertainties within the readings build up over time causing the output to be untrustworthy. Unlike the readings from the gyroscope, the magnetometer has an external reference. In the case of typical CubeSats, this is the Earth. By reading the magnetic fields produced by the Earth the sensor is able to calibrate itself relative to North which can be used to correct the drift from the gyroscope and provide a more accurate reading of the orientation of the satellite.

\subsection{Payload}

The payload subsystem is responsible for providing the user with the ability to create their own experiments and projects to integrate into the ORBITS unit. Rather than providing a specific payload for the system, the ORBITS unit is designed to be a flexible platform to enable users to create their own payloads.

The rapid prototyping of electronics is already a solved through the use of breadboards and perfboards. The payload subsystem provides a top layer development area for users to create their own payloads using these prototyping methods. The payload integrates a standard half-size breadboard and a set of headers connected to the OBC.

To allow for flexibility and enable creativity, a wide range of communication methods have been made available to use in communicating with the payload. General Purpose Input-Output pins is the most basic category, typically used for wiring up LEDs and transistors but they don't have an explicit purpose. I2C and SPI on the other hand are purpose made communication protocols. I2C focuses on reducing the number of pins required to communicate between a controller device and several peripherals through an address based system.

\subsection{Ground Station}
%Need to talk about the development of the software on the raspberry pi and add in software structure UML high level architecture probably
The ground station needs to facilitate multiple of the requirements needed for the system to be successful with particular focus on the educational element. The ground station is required to provide the link from a standard laptop or desktop environment to be able to develop 

The Raspberry Pi Zero 2W is a versatile single board computer that can have a wide range of different applications, in this case acting as the link between the users' development device and the ORBITS unit. With the versatility of such a device a wide range of different approaches can be taken to solve the ground station as many different design methodologies, frameworks and technologies that exist that can come together with differing results.

Commonly, Application Programming Interfaces (APIs) are used as a method for allowing different software components to interact with each other and is used within the ground station to allow for the frontend and backend to communicate with each other but there are several approaches that can be taken to implement an API in a greater tech stack.

%Add in a diagram showing each tech stack approaches
For instance, the MEAN framework (MongoDB, Express.js, Angular and Node.js) is an open source JavaScript framework that is mainly used for cloud based web applications~\cite{MEAN}. This is a highly scalable and flexible framework that can be used to create applications that serve many users at the same time. However, this framework may be overkill for the needs of the ORBITS system as it is designed for larger scale applications and may not be the best fit for a small scale project like this looking to individually serve users from the Raspberry Pi rather than a cloud based application.

In addition, a common tech stack used by hobbyists with the Raspberry Pi ecosystem of devices is LAMP, which stands for Linux, Apache, MySQL and Php or Python~\cite{LAMP}. This is a popular tech stack for web development and can be used to create a web application that serves the needs of the ORBITS system. The LAMP stack can be a good fit for the ORBITS system as it is designed for smaller scale applications and can be easily set up on the Raspberry Pi even with the limited resources available. However, this approach may not provide more modern features 

Finally, the approach taken for the ORBITS system, a more modern approach to web development is using a Flask backend with a React or AntDesign frontend. This approach allows for a more modern and responsive user interface while still being able to run on the Raspberry Pi. Flask is a lightweight web framework that can be used to create a RESTful API~\cite{REST_API} for the ground station while React or AntDesign can be used to create a user interface that interacts with the API. This approach requires more development time and resources but can provide a better user experience for users interacting with the ORBITS system and can allow the system to be more easily expanded in the future with additional features and functionality.

\subsubsection{Backend}
%WebSockets, SocketIO, Flask, ground station to unit comms thing you used, apache reverse proxy
%Need to first go into descriptions of each of the technologies used and then go into the architecture of how they all fit together.
The backend architecture for the ground station is built using several components with Flask being the main web framework used to create a RESTful API for the ground station.
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Flask:} Flask is a lightweight web framework for Python that allows for the creation of web applications and APIs. It is designed to be simple and easy to use, making it a good choice for small to medium sized projects. Flask provides a simple way to define routes and handle HTTP requests, making it easy to create a RESTful API for the ground station.
    \item \textbf{SocketIO:} SocketIO is a library that enables real-time, bidirectional communication between the client and the server. It is built on top of WebSockets and provides a simple API for handling real-time events.
    \item \textbf{Apache Reverse Proxy:} Apache is a widely used web server that can be configured to act as a reverse proxy. This allows for the backend Flask application to be served through the Apache web server, providing additional features such as load balancing and security. This is particularly useful to allow for the backend to be accessed from the local area network without needing to directly access the Raspberry Pi, which can be more difficult for users who are not familiar with networking and server administration.
    \item \textbf{MQTT:} MQTT is a lightweight messaging protocol that is designed for use in low bandwidth and high latency environments. It is often used in IoT applications and can be used to facilitate communication between the ground station and the ORBITS unit through a WiFi connection.
    \item \textbf{Arduino CLI:} This is a command line interface for the Arduino platform that allows for the compilation and uploading of code to Arduino compatible microcontrollers. This is used to compile the user defined code for the payload and upload it to the OBC microcontroller on the ORBITS unit through a feature called Over The Air (OTA) updates, which allows for the code to be uploaded wirelessly through the WiFi connection between the ground station and the ORBITS unit.
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection{Frontend}
%SocketIO, AntDesign, Blockly, Monaco Editor, React 
The frontend architecture for the ground station is build using React as the main framework for building the user interface. React provides a component-based architecture that allows for the creation of reusable UI components, making it easier to manage the complexity of the user interface. In addition to React, several libraries and frameworks are used to enhance the functionality of the frontend.
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{AntDesign:} AntDesign is a popular React UI library that provides a wide range of pre-built components and styles. It allows for the creation of a modern and responsive user interface with minimal effort.
    \item \textbf{Blockly:} Blockly is a library for building visual programming editors. It allows users to create code by dragging and dropping blocks that represent different programming constructs. This can be particularly useful for users who are new to programming or prefer a visual approach to coding.
    \item \textbf{Monaco Editor:} Monaco Editor is a code editor that is used in Visual Studio Code. In this case it is used to provide the code being generated by the Blockly editor in a text format that can be used for debugging and to bridge the gap between the visual programming and traditional text based programming.
    \item \textbf{SocketIO:} SocketIO is used in the frontend to enable real-time communication with the backend Flask application. This allows for real-time updates to the user interface based on events that occur in the backend, such as receiving telemetry data from the ORBITS unit or sending commands to the unit.
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection{Database}
%Mimicking actual telemetry data from the ISS mainly from formatting and include schema for the database and phpmyadmin. 
The database within the ground station is used to store general system operations and telemetry from the ORBITS unit, rather than storing it on board. The database contains 3 separate tables:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{System Events:} General operations of the device, for instance changing operating mode, charging state or error.
    \item \textbf{Telemetry Updates:} Recurring data coming from the onboard sensors, Not as frequent as that of the ADCS. For instance, voltage and current draw or Payload readings.
    \item \textbf{ADCS Data:} Used to store frequent readings from the Attitude Determination and Control system 
\end{itemize}

These tables follow a similar structure to that of the telemetry data from the ISS. A subset of this data is available through both the ISS Mimic~\cite{ISS-Mimic} project and \href{https://lightstreamer.com/}{Lightstreamer}, and this informed the design of the database.

The system events tables are structured as such:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{ID:} Standard automatic incrementing integer primary key.
    \item \textbf{Mission ID:} Unique Identifier to distinguish user variants with different code implementations within the structure.
    \item \textbf{Event Type:} General category in which an operation has occurred like payload operation.
    \item \textbf{Description:} Short description of the operation occurred.
    \item \textbf{Timestamp:} Time at which the operation occurred from on board real-time clock.
\end{itemize}

Finally, the ADCS updates has its own table structured differently. This is to service high request volumes in comparison to the other tables.

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{ID:} Standard auto increment primary key.
    \item \textbf{Mission ID:} Unique Identifier to distinguish different code implementations.
    \item \textbf{Acceleration:} 3 components each corresponding to the acceleration in each axis.
    \item \textbf{Gyroscope:} 3 components representing the Roll, Pitch, and Yaw of the ORBITS unit at a given time.
    \item \textbf{Magnetometer:} 3 components representing the magnetic field strength in each of the 3 axis.
    \item \textbf{Timestamp:} The corresponding time measured by the on board real-time clock.
\end{itemize}
